Overview

1Executive Summary

Spans Ventures Limited is pleased to propose the design and deployment of a modern, scalable Fibre-to-the-Home (FTTH) broadband network across two Braeburn residential estates.

Following our site survey of 12 June 2026, both sites have been confirmed as technically feasible greenfield deployments. We propose a passive optical (GPON) architecture at each site, delivering dedicated high-speed fibre broadband to every apartment, together with managed Wi-Fi coverage of the shared outdoor areas.

Site A · BGR

Braeburn Gitanga Road — 4 blocks, 24 homes. Aerial ADSS feed from a nearby utility pole to an OLT head-end, distributing one signal per block.

Site B · BGE

Braeburn Garden Estate — 2 blocks, 32 homes. Centralised server-room head-end (OLT + Mikrotik) feeding front & back FATs per block.

The combined network serves 56 homes using standards-based GPON equipment, all-dielectric (ADSS) aerial cable, armoured drop cabling and weatherproof terminations — engineered for reliability, easy maintenance and future scalability (VoIP, IPTV, CCTV).

The brief

3Project Understanding & Scope

Braeburn requires reliable, individually-metered fibre broadband to every apartment across the two estates, plus Wi-Fi coverage of the common recreation areas. Both sites are greenfield — no existing structured cabling or active network infrastructure was observed during the survey.

Scope of supply & works

  • Detailed network design and as-built documentation for both sites.
  • Supply of all passive and active equipment (OLT, routers, splitters, FATs, ONUs, cabling, enclosures).
  • Aerial (ADSS) and in-building fibre routing, splicing, termination and labelling.
  • Drop-cable installation to each apartment, terminating at an Access Terminal Box (ATB) and ONU.
  • Outdoor access point installation for common-area Wi-Fi.
  • Testing (OTDR / power-meter), commissioning and handover.

Out of scope

  • Internet bandwidth / ISP service subscription (to be contracted separately by Braeburn).
  • Civil trenching beyond corridor/surface conduit, and mains electrical works to equipment rooms.
  • In-apartment furniture relocation or making good of decorative finishes.
How we build it

4Technical Approach & Standards

Both networks use a GPON passive optical architecture: a single OLT head-end feeds passive optical splitters, which fan out to Fibre Access Terminals (FATs) and then to each home — no active electronics between the head-end and the apartment, which maximises reliability and minimises maintenance.

Split architecture

A 1/4 primary split at the head-end creates one dedicated signal per FAT. Each 8-port FAT carries a 1/8 splitter, serving up to 8 homes. End-to-end ratio: 1:32.

Cable & protection

ADSS all-dielectric aerial cable for external spans (no metallic messenger, reduced lightning risk); armoured drop cable in corridors; weatherproof wall-mount enclosures for all FATs/FTPs.

The last mile — into every home

From the FAT, an individual drop cable runs to each apartment and terminates at a wall-mounted Access Terminal Box (ATB). A short patch connects the ATB to the ONU/ONT, which converts fibre to Ethernet/Wi-Fi for the resident's router. This chain — FAT → drop cable → ATB → ONU → router — is identical at both sites and is illustrated in the topology diagrams that follow.

Standards & good practice: all fibre, FATs and closures labelled to a Block/Apartment/Port convention; exposed runs in rigid PVC/HDPE conduit; OTDR and optical-power testing on every link prior to handover.
Site A

Site typeResidential apartment complex — 4 blocks, linear arrangement
Homes24 apartments (6 per block)
Fibre entryUtility pole ≈ 50 m from compound perimeter (ADSS aerial)
Head-endOLT + router in the Block 2 equipment room (Laundry Room)

Existing conditions

Greenfield site — no existing cabling. A concrete utility pole ≈ 50 m from the boundary is suitable for ADSS stringing. Staircase corridors between blocks provide viable routing pathways; the Block 2 Laundry Room offers space for active equipment (subject to confirmed power and ventilation).

Proposed topology

Live interactive diagram (also available as the standalone file bgr.html):

The internet feed arrives via ADSS from the utility pole into the OLT. The OLT performs the 1/4 primary split, sending four dedicated signals — one to each block's 8-port FAT. Each FAT (1/8 splitter) serves that block's 6 homes via individual drop cables to the ATB and ONU, leaving 2 spare ports per FAT for future growth. A PoE switch off the OLT feeds the outdoor common-area access point.

Site B

6Braeburn Garden Estate · BGE

Site typeResidential apartment complex — 2 blocks
Homes32 apartments (16 per block — 8 front + 8 back)
Head-endDedicated server room — OLT + Mikrotik router (central hub)
TopologyHub-and-spoke (star) — every block served directly from the server room
Distribution1/4 primary split → front & back 8-port FAT (1/8) per block
Total split1:32 — one OLT PON port serves all 32 homes

Existing conditions

Greenfield site with a significant advantage: a dedicated server room with space for a rack, patch panel and end closure. Both blocks are structurally accessible from the server room, supporting a clean centralised hub-and-spoke distribution and secure, manageable termination.

Why front & back FATs

Each block houses 16 apartments — 8 at the front and 8 at the back. Since a single 8-port FAT (1/8 splitter) serves exactly 8 homes, each block is served by two FATs: one for the front 8 and one for the back 8. Two blocks therefore require four FATs, fed by the OLT's 1/4 split. At 1:32 the network is fully loaded; 12/16-port FATs are recommended if spare capacity is required.

Proposed topology

Live interactive diagram (also available as the standalone file bge.html):

The feed enters the server room where the Mikrotik handles routing/gateway duties and the OLT performs GPON distribution. Four dedicated signals leave the OLT to the front and back FATs of each block; each FAT serves its 8 homes via drop cable → ATB → ONU. A PoE switch feeds the common-area outdoor AP.
